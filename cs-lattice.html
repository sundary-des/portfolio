Lattice Studio — designing an AI product builder for digital creators

Product Design · AI Platform · 2025

Lattice Studio needed a guided creation flow that made AI feel like a collaborator, not a black box — so creators could go from idea to a published, storefront-ready digital product in less than a minute.

Project snapshot

Product: Lattice Studio — an AI Product Builder within the Lattice AI Tools Suite

My role: Product Owner + UX/UI Designer. Discovery, requirements, end-to-end UX, UI execution, design system, and QA.

The brief: Build the core creation experience for a tool that lets digital creators (Etsy sellers, Gumroad authors, Teachable instructors) generate complete, sellable digital products — course, pack, podcast, challenge — and publish them directly into their existing storefronts via link or embed code.

The project reached full production-ready delivery. The decision not to ship was the client's.

The Problem

Digital creators who already run storefronts face a specific friction point: building a new digital product — even a simple mini-course or resource pack — typically takes several days and requires coordinating at least four separate tools: writing, design, file packaging, listing copy. For creators who aren't technical, it's slow, inconsistent, and discouraging.

User conversations revealed three consistent blockers:

  • Blank page paralysis. Creators knew what they wanted to teach but couldn't structure it into a sellable format on their own. They stall before they start.
  • Toolchain fragmentation. Content, cover image, listing copy, and pricing each lived in a different tool. Any change cascaded across all of them.
  • Pre-publish anxiety. Creators couldn't see what buyers would actually see until the product was already live — so many drafts never shipped.

The design goal wasn't to build a powerful builder. It was to make the process feel guided, reversible, and visible at every step.

By the numbers

Digital creators using disconnected tool stacks spend an estimated 40–60% of their working time on non-revenue tasks — formatting, file preparation, and listing creation. Tools that consolidate creation into a single guided flow show 2–3× faster time-to-publish compared to multi-tool workflows. The blank page problem is the #1 self-reported abandonment trigger in creation flows — cited by the majority of creators who start a product draft but never complete it.

What I designed

The complete Lattice Studio creation interface, showing all three tools in the suite and the 5-step guided wizard.

The 5-step wizard

The core of Lattice Studio is a split-screen, step-by-step creation flow. The left panel is always the active editing step. The right panel is always a live preview — updating in real time as the product takes shape.

Each step resolves one question at a time: what type of product, what content goes in it, what bonuses to include, how it's priced, and finally a pre-publish review before it goes live. The sequence is linear but reversible — creators can step back at any point without losing their work.

1 Product type selection

The first step asks one question: what kind of product do you want to create? Mini-course, resource pack, challenge, or podcast. Keeping this choice visible and named — rather than hidden behind a dropdown — reduces decision fatigue and anchors the creator's mental model for every step that follows.

Each type opens a slightly different path through the wizard. The product type defines the default content structure AI generates in Step 2 — so the choice here has downstream consequences that the creator never has to manually manage.

2 AI-generated content structure

The blank page problem is solved here. After entering a topic and audience, the AI generates a full product outline — modules, lesson names, estimated time, and file counts — instantly. The creator reviews, edits, and reorders rather than writes from scratch.

This is the most trust-critical step in the flow. The AI output had to feel specific and useful, not generic and discardable. Prompting and output formatting were iterated extensively based on session feedback — creators needed to feel like the AI understood their niche, not just the category.

3 Bonus materials

Bonuses are a standard conversion lever in digital product storefronts — they increase perceived value without increasing production complexity. Step 3 lets creators add pre-built bonus types (cheat sheet, printable, template) with AI-generated content for each.

Each bonus shows an estimated value, visible in the live preview panel. This isn't cosmetic — it directly informs the pricing decision in Step 4 and gives creators a concrete way to anchor their product's perceived worth before they've set a price.

4 Pricing configuration

Pricing is where creators most often second-guess themselves. Step 4 surfaces the total perceived value (sum of content + bonuses) alongside the price input, so creators have a concrete reference point rather than guessing in isolation.

The interface also supports a launch discount — a time-limited reduced price shown in the live preview as a strikethrough original price and discount badge. This isn't a feature added for completeness; it's the specific pricing pattern that drives first-sale conversion in digital storefronts.

5 Cover image & listing copy

Before review, the AI generates the storefront-facing assets: a product title, subtitle, short description, and cover image — all pulled from the product structure defined in Steps 1–3. The creator edits rather than writes.

Cover image generation was the most technically constrained step. The output needed to look credible on Etsy, Gumroad, and Teachable thumbnails simultaneously — different aspect ratios, different visual conventions. The design resolved this with a square-first approach that crops gracefully across all platforms.

6 Review & publish

The final creation step before publish is a structured review: a product summary card (type, modules, bonuses, price), a module-by-module product details panel, and a pre-publish checklist showing completion status for each section.

The checklist isn't a gate — it's a confidence mechanism. Every item checked signals to the creator that they haven't missed anything, and gives them permission to publish without second-guessing. The "Publish product" CTA is only visible once all checklist items pass, removing ambiguity about when the product is truly ready.

7 Customer view mode

At any point during the creation flow, a "View as customer" toggle switches the right panel from the live build preview into a buyer-facing storefront simulation — showing exactly what a buyer on Gumroad or Etsy would see.

This feature directly addresses pre-publish anxiety. Creators who can see the buyer's view before going live are more likely to commit to publishing. The toggle is always one click away, never a separate mode — so the cost of checking is near zero.

8 Storefront embed & publish

After publishing, Lattice generates a direct link and an embed code the creator pastes into their existing storefront. There's no Lattice-hosted store — the product lives where the creator's audience already is.

This was a deliberate product constraint that shaped several design decisions upstream. Because the output had to work in multiple storefronts, every generated asset — cover image, listing copy, pricing display — was designed against the lowest common denominator of all supported platforms.

Core Design Decisions

1. Live preview as a trust mechanism, not a feature

The right-panel live preview isn't decorative. It exists because the biggest creator fear is "I don't know what I'm actually publishing." Making the customer view permanently visible — before, during, and after every edit — removed the need for a separate preview mode and reduced the anxiety that kills commitment.

This isn't a UX convenience. It's the primary confidence lever in the entire flow.

By the numbers

In usability studies of multi-step creation tools, real-time output preview reduces task abandonment by 20–30% compared to flows where users only see results after completion. When users can continuously validate what they're building, they make faster decisions and show significantly lower drop-off at the final commitment step. Perceived control — not actual control — is the primary driver of completion in guided creation flows.

2. One question per step, no exceptions

Every step in the wizard resolves exactly one decision: product type, content structure, bonuses, pricing, or review. Combining any two steps — even ones that seem related — consistently caused creators to pause, re-read, and second-guess.

The split-screen layout reinforces this. The left panel changes with each step; the right panel never does. Spatial consistency gives creators a mental anchor: "I'm always editing on the left, always seeing the result on the right." This reduced instruction-reading and support questions in every usability round.

3. The pre-publish checklist as a commitment device

The final step includes a pre-publish checklist — four items that mirror the four previous creation steps. Each item shows a green check once the corresponding step is complete. The "Publish" button is visible throughout, but the checklist gives creators the psychological permission to use it.

The checklist isn't about completeness verification — the system already knows whether each section is filled. It's about making the creator feel seen: "You did the work. You're ready." That shift from doubt to confidence is what turns a draft into a published product.

4. AI as a starting point, not a black box

Every AI-generated output in Lattice Studio is immediately editable. The content structure, bonus descriptions, listing copy, and cover image can all be regenerated, replaced, or manually overwritten — in the same view where they appear, without a separate editing mode.

This matters because creators have strong opinions about their content. An AI output that feels imposed will be rejected or worked around. An AI output that feels like a draft to improve will be adopted. The interaction model — generate, then edit in place — was the mechanism that made AI feel like a collaborator rather than a decision-maker.
